Vision Fidelity: How We Deepened Six Mythological Species

We went back to the source material and found that our implementations had drifted. Not wrong, exactly. But thinner than what the traditions actually describe. So we fixed it.

MVEE has a lot of species. Each one is supposed to carry the weight of the mythology it came from — not just the aesthetics, but the logic. The social structures, the ritual systems, the reasons a tradition developed the way it did.

We ran a vision fidelity audit this week. We took every species in the game, compared its implementation against the Akashic Records — our canonical lore source, built from primary ethnographic and theological texts — and asked a simple question: does this species behave the way its source tradition says it should?

Six species came back with gaps worth closing.

What We Found

Jiangshi-Vel — hopping vampires from Chinese folklore. We had the movement right. We had the qi-drain right. What we were missing was the infrastructure. In the source tradition, communities don't just fight Jiangshi. They build entire systems around managing the improperly dead: corpse-driver logistics routes through mountain passes, Mao Shan Taoist specialists whose social standing rises and falls with outbreak frequency, glutinous rice barriers maintained like public utilities. Death care isn't a side quest. It's load-bearing infrastructure. We built that out.

Sachamama-Vel — serpent guardians from Andean Quechua tradition. The original implementation treated them as territorial predators. The source material describes something more complex: beings that are part of the landscape itself, whose presence indicates ecological health. We deepened their relationship with the land systems so their behavior reflects stewardship, not just aggression.

Djinn-Ahl — beings of smokeless fire. The theological framework was there in skeleton form. We filled it out: the contract-based society where every relationship is a negotiated covenant, the 72 phase-states that determine social role and compatibility, the conviction that the universe itself is a negotiation between forces. For the Djinn-Ahl, breaking a promise isn't dishonest. It's structurally dangerous.

Tengu-Ra, Kappa, and Kumiho — from Japanese and Korean mythology. Each had functional mechanics but was missing the cultural specificity that makes the traditions distinct. The Tengu's relationship with mountain asceticism, the Kappa's water-rights negotiation systems, the Kumiho's shape-shifting as social commentary rather than predatory trick. We went deeper into the source texts and let the mechanics follow.

The Other Change

Holy texts used to be generated from templates. A civilization would reach a certain threshold and receive a procedurally assembled scripture from a pool of fragments. It worked, technically. But the texts read like they were written by someone who had never lived in the civilization that was supposed to believe them.

Now holy texts are generated from what actually happened. The events, the schisms, the moral crises that a species community went through — those become the raw material. The moral framework of the species shapes how those events are interpreted. Two species can live through the same drought and produce completely different scriptures about what it meant.

The schisms are real too. When a community's moral framework encounters an event that doesn't fit, the community splits. Not because we scripted a split. Because the moral logic produced genuine disagreement.

Why This Matters

It would have been easy to leave these species at the level they were at. They functioned. Players could interact with them. But the point of building from folklore isn't to borrow names and silhouettes. It's to encode the logic of traditions that real communities developed over centuries — and to let that logic produce emergent behavior that we couldn't have designed from scratch.

A Jiangshi-Vel outbreak that cascades because a community underinvested in burial rites isn't a scripted event. It's the tradition's own logic, playing out in a simulated world. That's what vision fidelity means to us: the source material isn't decoration. It's the engine.
