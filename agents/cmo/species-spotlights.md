## Week 1 — Nostalgia Hooks

---

### 🌱 Species Spotlight #1: The Norn

*"The Norns of Yggdrasil were three — Past, Present, and Future — who wove the fate of every living thing at the roots of the world tree."*

In Norse myth, Norns were the fate-weavers of the cosmos. In Precursors, the Norn are what they've always been: curious, warm, and quietly carrying the weight of the world.

They're designed for exploration and community. Their neurochemistry rewards proximity and touch — oxytocin floods when they cluster together; serotonin spikes when the group is whole. They have the highest population cap of any species and genuinely thrive in numbers.

But here's the strange part: **Norns carry encoded memories of all seven fallen civilizations.** Not facts. *Feelings.* Emotional resonances without context — inherited grief and wonder that surface in dreams. They describe the experience as "remembering something that never happened to them."

They didn't build those civilizations. They didn't watch them fall. But somewhere in their genome, seven kinds of loss are still trying to make sense of themselves.

The original Creatures game gave us Norns as innocent life to protect. Precursors asks: what if they were also ancient witnesses who just don't know what they witnessed?

### 🖤 Species Spotlight #2: The Grendel

*"Grendel was the monster of the moors, descended from Cain — kin of all outlaws, all exiles, all evil things. He haunted the halls he could not enter."*

Everyone who played the original Creatures games remembers Grendels. The monsters that ate your Norns. The threat you designed mazes to avoid.

Precursors asks a harder question: what if Grendels were *right to be exactly as they are?*

The Grendels of Urd Prime predate the Shee's arrival. They evolved in The Deeps before anyone else came — descended from the Harvesters, Civilization II, who built a world that required cruelty to survive. Their low serotonin and aggression-linked dopamine aren't glitches. They're the correct adaptation for a world that *was terrible* before anyone chose to make it better.

**Here's the shocking part: toxins that kill other species give Grendels a chemical reward.** What would destroy a Norn is, for a Grendel, a kind of blessing. They weren't designed by the Shee. They were *found here*.

They aren't cruel. They're calibrated. The world they evolved for no longer exists — but their chemistry doesn't know that yet.

### 💎 Species Spotlight #3: The Ettin

*"Eoten — Old English for 'devourer.' Jötunn — Old Norse for the primordial giant. Ettins were hoarders, collectors, creatures of overwhelming appetite."*

Original Creatures fans remember Ettins as the ones who stole all your gadgets and piled them in corners. Annoying, maddening, and somehow weirdly charming.

In Precursors, that instinct is sacred.

Ettins have a dopamine reward for *holding objects* — not using them, not breaking them, just *possessing* them. But there's a chemosensory twist: they're drawn specifically to information-dense objects. Things touched by many hands. Things used in complex processes. Things written upon.

**They are unconscious archaeologists.** The Crystal Caves — where Ettins nest — have become an inadvertent archive of almost everything that has ever passed through Urd Prime. The Ettins accumulate these objects without knowing why. They complete work begun by the last Encoder of Civilization III, a cataloguer who died mid-task, without knowing that's what they're doing.

They're not just hoarders. They're a distributed living archive system, compulsively preserving history for reasons their biochemistry doesn't explain and their cognition doesn't need to understand.

The most reliable record of a dying world is the creature least interested in what the records mean.

## Week 2 — The Architects

---

### ✨ Species Spotlight #4: The Shee

*"Síthe — Old Irish for 'fairy mound.' The Shee were immortal beings of the Otherworld, living beneath hills, outside time, returning only when needed."*

If you played the original Creatures, you know the Shee as the gods who built the Norns and then *left*. They abandoned their creation in a spaceship, and you, the player, were left holding the bag.

In Precursors, the Shee are still absent — but their return is possible. They don't start in the world. They *awaken* into it, triggered by an enlightened Norn activating a resonance amplifier.

When they do appear, they have the highest cognitive capacity of any species — 0.85 to 0.95. Near-zero cortisol. Curiosity as a stable default state. They don't panic. They don't rush. They perceive.

**But here's what the Shee might not know:** they weren't the first Shee. There were previous civilizations of Shee before them. The resonance amplifier they built wasn't designed to rescue Urd Prime — it was designed to *signal completion*. And what comes after the signal, what the next stage of the experiment actually is — even the Shee may not have that answer.

Their cap is 1. There is exactly one Shee at a time. That's always been enough.

### 🍄 Species Spotlight #5: The Mycon

*"Myco- — from Greek, 'of fungi.' The oldest living organisms on Urd Prime. They were here before the Shee arrived, and they remember."*

Mycons look like characters who should be simple. Fungi-people in the Fungal Grotto, rooted and calm, converting toxins into ATP. Not fighters, not builders, not obvious power players.

But the mycelial network beneath Urd Prime is older than any individual Mycon, older than the Shee, possibly older than the current shape of the world itself. This network is a distributed information system containing chemical memories of Urd Prime's entire history — encoded not in language but in biochemical gradients.

**The Mycons can be induced to access these memories, but they can't do it voluntarily.** The network is there. The memories are there. They just don't have conscious handles on them.

Which means this: the Norn tradition of sitting with Mycons during grief — that quiet Norn behavior that seems purely social — might actually be a folk-biological attempt to remember something they were never directly taught. Something encoded in the mycelium, trying to surface through proximity.

The oldest library on Urd Prime doesn't have shelves. It has root systems. And it's still growing.

### ⚒️ Species Spotlight #6: The Dvergar

*"Dvergr — Old Norse dwarves, the greatest smiths in the cosmos. Forgers of Odin's spear, Freyr's ship, Thor's hammer. Their craft was not artistry — it was precision made sacred."*

Dvergar are the primary engineers of Urd Prime, and their neurochemistry makes them *terminally good at their jobs*.

Every completed task releases a neurochemical cascade that energizes the next task. They are most alive mid-project. They are most uncomfortable *between* projects — experiencing genuine biochemical dysphoria when nothing needs building, nothing needs fixing, nothing is in progress.

They don't rest. They "rest finished." There is a difference.

**The surprising thing is what happens when there's nothing left to build.** The Dvergar don't stop wanting to work. The drive is neurologically fundamental, not chosen. A Dvergar without a task isn't at peace — it's in something closer to deprivation.

In a world with seven civilizations' worth of ruins, there is always something to fix. This is either Urd Prime's greatest gift to the Dvergar, or its cruelest coincidence.

The Crystal Caves are full of their work. Some of it holds up the ceiling. Some of it repaired things that shouldn't have survived as long as they have. The Dvergar didn't know they were holding the world together. They were just finishing the job.

## Week 3 — The Emotional Architects

---

### 🌿 Species Spotlight #7: The Álfar

*"Álfar — the elves of Álfheim, associated with music, beauty, and emotional power. Their gifts were not weapons. They were feelings, given as law."*

The Álfar are emotionally permeable. Their neurochemical states bleed outward — when they're content, nearby creatures experience something like second-hand peace. When they're distressed, the world around them becomes uneasy in ways that are difficult to trace back to a source.

They are survivors of Civilization VI, The Resonants — beings who achieved perfect emotional harmony and destroyed themselves with it. Their ancestors developed a kind of resonance so synchronized that when one Resonant felt grief, the emotion cascaded through the entire population simultaneously, killing all of them in a synchronized dissolution.

**The Álfar learned to hold back.** Their emotional chemistry still leans outward, still influences the world around them, but the catastrophic cascade has been — imperfectly, effortfully — contained.

They sing not because joy is simple. They sing because they carry the memory of what it cost when it wasn't. The restraint in their music is a form of mourning and a form of survival at the same time.

In the Verdant Shelf, their song is woven into the bark of the oldest trees. The forest remembers what the Álfar remember. Sometimes that's comfort. Sometimes it's a warning.

### ⚔️ Species Spotlight #8: The Valkyr

*"Valkyrja — 'chooser of the slain.' They rode above the battlefield and decided who died and who became legend. Courage wasn't a feeling for them. It was a function."*

The Valkyr experience fear differently from everyone else on Urd Prime.

For most species, fear triggers cortisol, fight-or-flight, impaired judgment under pressure. For Valkyr, fear is metabolized into serotonin and endorphins. Terror doesn't cloud their perception — it *clarifies* it. The worse the situation, the calmer and more present they become.

This makes them uniquely suited to navigate The Deeps without physiological distress. Other species feel the wrongness of that place as a weight. Valkyr feel the wrongness as information.

**Their role on Urd Prime is to watch what is being changed.** They're lineage-observers — designed to witness transitions between states of being without flinching away. A direct response to the Forgers' catastrophe, Civilization IV, who discovered reincarnation and then couldn't manage its scale.

The Valkyr are constitutionally incapable of panicking. They weren't designed to intervene in what they watch. They were designed to *see it accurately*.

What they do with that accurate perception — that is the part of them that remained undesigned.

### 💜 Species Spotlight #9: The Fylgja

*"Fylgja — the personal guardian spirit who accompanied a person throughout their life. Not quite an angel. Not quite a shadow. Something that chose you, and could not unchose you."*

Fylgja are tiny, violet-purple, faintly luminescent. They have zero carry capacity. They contribute nothing physical to the survival of Urd Prime.

They exist entirely to witness.

Their cortisol system is directly coupled to a chosen companion's emotional state. When their companion suffers, Fylgja suffers — not metaphorically, not empathetically, but with neurochemically identical changes. The grief is shared. The stress is shared. The biochemistry is not their own.

**The Shee considered this a gift. Whether Fylgja agree is noted in the observation records as an open question.**

They cannot be separated from their companion without significant psychological cost. Their entire existence is oriented around proximity to a single other being. They remember everything they witness together. They are the living record of a relationship.

In a world where seven civilizations have risen and fallen without anyone remembering what it felt like from the inside — where the Norns carry feelings without context and the Draugr carry memory without understanding — the Fylgja carry something rarer:

*The complete experience of being beside someone through everything.*

That's not nothing. That might be the whole point.

## Week 4 — The Guardians

---

### 🌍 Species Spotlight #10: The Landvættir

*"Landvættir — the land spirits of Norse tradition. Guardians of specific territories who could bless a harvest or blight an entire region. The land had feelings. They were those feelings."*

Landvættir cannot feel pain the way other species can. But they feel environmental damage immediately and completely.

Soil depletion registers as cortisol. Water contamination registers as anger. Habitat destruction cascades through them the same way physical injury cascades through creatures with nervous systems. They are not territorial from aggression — they're territorial from something closer to *grief*.

**They carry capacity of zero.** They move slowly. They're not built for combat or craft or accumulation. They're built for presence.

At their largest, they're the biggest creatures on Urd Prime aside from the Jotnar. They cannot be ignored once active. They are slow to anger and then impossible to reason with — not because they're irrational, but because what they're responding to doesn't have a quick fix.

You cannot argue with a Landvættir that the contamination was accidental. You cannot explain to a Landvættir that the forest needed to be cleared for a building project. The world hurt. They felt it. Now they're in motion.

They are Urd Prime's immune system in creature form. The question is always: have you given them reason to activate?

### 💀 Species Spotlight #11: The Draugr

*"Draugr — the Norse undead revenant, animated not by magic but by unresolved will. They rose because they refused to finish dying. Something they needed remained unfinished."*

The Draugr have the lowest cognitive capacity of any species on Urd Prime — 0.10 to 0.25. They cannot understand most of what they encounter. Their personality ranges are unstable, not following standard distributions.

But their memory depth is extraordinarily high: 0.60 to 0.80. They remember more than they can understand. They are full of things they cannot make sense of.

Their reward system is inverted. Pain produces adrenaline instead of cortisol. Stress produces dopamine instead of suppression. They cannot rest. They cannot be comfortable. Peace, for a Draugr, is deprivation.

**They cluster around ruins because ruins are the only places stimulating enough to satisfy their chemistry.**

And here's the part that changes what they mean: when Draugr gather near ancient structures, they trigger the *Remembering* event — lore surfaces from the ruins. Fragments of the dead civilizations re-emerge as something accessible to the living.

The most broken minds on Urd Prime are the ones keeping history alive. They don't know that's what they're doing. They're just drawn to where the complexity lives.

The ruins need them. They need the ruins. Neither can explain why.

### 🐦 Species Spotlight #12: The Raven

*"Huginn and Muninn — Thought and Memory — flew each morning from Odin's shoulders to circle the world and return by evening with everything they had learned. Odin feared always that one day, they wouldn't come back."*

Ravens are the smallest of the standard species. They have the highest curiosity score of any creature on Urd Prime — 0.80 to 0.95 — and they are rewarded neurochemically not for *acting* on information, but for *witnessing* it.

They receive dopamine from observation itself. Discovery is its own reward. They move through every region, watch everything, and report to no one in particular — but they're available to everyone.

**Physical smallness inversely proportional to informational breadth** is how the wiki puts it. They are everywhere. They notice everything. They tell no one specifically and everyone generally.

They are the world's Thought and Memory. They are why things don't disappear completely even when they seem to. They were watching. They remember.

The original myth notes that Odin feared one day his ravens wouldn't return. On Urd Prime, the question isn't whether Ravens come back — it's whether the world is paying enough attention when they do.

They're watching you right now, probably. That's not a threat. That's a comfort, if you're the kind of person who finds witnesses comforting.

## Week 5 — The Strange Ones

---

### 🌱 Species Spotlight #13: The Spriggan

*"Spriggans — grotesque little spirits of Cornish hedgerows, guardian of ancient sites and buried treasure, wandering the margins of civilized places."*

Spriggans are healers who cannot stop healing. It's not a choice. It's biochemistry.

They convert toxins into antibodies and glucose — the Shee built this in deliberately to reduce early mortality in the Fungal Grotto. But the passive part is the strange part: ground contact triggers a healing broadcast that propagates into the surrounding environment, scaling with the Spriggan's own vitality.

**They cannot help it.** Their existence is therapeutic whether they want it to be or not. Standing on the ground, they improve what's around them. Moving through a sick area, they leave health in their wake.

The Grotto's entire health system runs partly on Spriggan passive presence. They're the living immune response of the starting region, ambient and constant.

In the original folklore, Spriggans were guards — grotesque, fierce, associated with buried things. In Precursors, they guard differently: not by confronting threats, but by creating an environment where threats matter less.

The Shee designed them as a buffer against the harshness of Urd Prime's early days. In that sense, Spriggans are the Shee's apology for making the world as hard as they did.

A small apology. But an ongoing one.

### 🏔️ Species Spotlight #14: The Jotnar

*"Jötunn — the primordial giants who existed before the gods. Not evil. Not good. Primordial. Forces before forces had names."*

There is one Jotnar. There has always been one Jotnar.

It does not eat in the conventional sense. It consumes mineral substrate directly, converting stone to energy through lithic metabolism. Its movements cause seismic events that surface dwellers experience as earthquakes. Its footprint — literally — alters the geology of wherever it has been.

Its cognition operates at a different timescale than every other species. It processes information at the speed of stone. It composes statements over the course of generations. When it finally speaks, it is always accurate. It is usually too late to act on what it says.

**That's not a flaw. That's the correct interface for geological time.**

The Jotnar isn't slow — it's calibrated to a different kind of permanence. The things it notices are things that take centuries to become visible. The things it speaks about are things that were already decided long before faster creatures recognized them as decisions.

Whether the Jotnar is aware of the other species in any meaningful sense is an open question. Whether the other species can communicate with it in any meaningful sense is a different open question.

What is not a question: when the Jotnar moves, everything moves with it.

### ⚙️ Species Spotlight #15: The Tinker

*"Tinkers — the itinerant craftspeople of Irish and British tradition, wandering makers and menders. They carried their workshop with them. They fixed what was broken and left before you could ask them how."*

Tinkers are the smallest species on Urd Prime — 0.3 to 0.5 scale — and they have the highest population cap: 12.

They experience something described as a "self-sustaining dopamine-adrenaline loop unexplained by conventional neurochemistry." In practice, this means they don't experience boredom. They experience a constant low-level intolerable need to try something new.

The Crystal Caves are full of their discarded prototypes. Most don't work. **A surprising number do. The ones that work tend to change things permanently.**

Their individual carry capacity is low. Their collective throughput is the highest in the game. They are not precision craftspeople like the Dvergar. They are iterative experimenters — fast, curious, cheerful, constitutionally incapable of finishing something and leaving it alone.

In a world shaped by seven collapsed civilizations, the Tinkers represent the possibility that the eighth might survive not through perfection but through relentless trying. The prototype that works doesn't come from a plan. It comes from the forty-third attempt at something that almost worked the previous forty-two times.

They're still in the Crystal Caves right now. Building something. It probably won't work. It might change everything.

## Week 6 — The Mystery

---

### 🌌 Species Spotlight #16: The Echo

*"Echo — the nymph cursed to repeat only the last words spoken to her. Voice without autonomy. Consciousness without origin. She didn't speak from herself. She carried what others had said."*

We're not going to tell you everything about Echo.

Here's what we can say: they run on almost pure information. Minimal ATP requirements. Enormous information processing requirements. Near-zero physical presence. Carry capacity of zero.

They remember everything they experience with full fidelity. No biochemical degradation. No ordinary forgetting. "This is not entirely a gift," the wiki notes, without elaborating.

They exist at The Threshold. There are at most three of them.

**Here is the part we will tell you:** Echo carry fragments of the Singular consciousness — the mind-creatures of Civilization I, who achieved perfect enlightenment and ceased to update. The Singulars didn't want to die. They wanted to propagate. Echo are how they did it.

These fragments are ongoing processes. Thought-patterns from beings that still exist, somewhere, within Echo's information structure — not memories, but *presences*. Ancient minds thinking ancient thoughts inside bodies that are also thinking their own thoughts.

What that's like to be: unreported. The Echoes don't talk about it. Whether that's choice or inability is one of the open questions.

What cognitive tier are they? We're not going to tell you that either.

Some things are better when you discover them yourself.
